## 2. Removing the Friction: The Web of Trust Architecture
The "Problem of the Intermediary" is solved by building a visible, high-trust social graph. We don't create trust; we provide the tools to reveal the trust that already exists in the neighborhood.

### 2.1 The KULA Mycelium (Visualizing Indirect Bonds)
We often feel like strangers in our own cities, yet we are connected by a vast, invisible network of mutual friends, shared organizations, and neighborhood history. We call this the **KULA Mycelium**.
*   **The Visualization:** KULA maps these "Indirect Bonds," showing you that the person offering a tool three streets away is your "Neighbor's-Neighbor" or a fellow member of the same local garden.
*   **The Psychological Shift:** Seeing the "Invisible Thread" that connects you to a stranger removes the friction of the "Ask." You aren't asking a stranger; you are asking a part of your extended social organism.

### 2.2 The Mechanics of Connection
*   **Invitation Codes (Lineage):** Every member enters through an invitation. This creates a "Lineage Tree" that maps how the community grows. Your code isn't just a key; it is your social DNA within the network.
*   **Circles (The Neighborhood Cell):** Circles are hyper-local nodes (e.g., "Mitte North" or "Kottbusser Tor Neighbors") where the Mycelium is densest.
*   **Organizations (The Anchor Nodes):** Local bookstores, community centers, and urban gardens act as institutional anchors. They bridge multiple circles and provide "Old Growth" stability to the network.
*   **Trustee Requests (Active Vouching):** Beyond simple following, users can send "Trustee Requests." This is a formal "Vouch" where one neighbor takes social responsibility for another, strengthening the bond between disparate branches of the Mycelium.

### 2.3 Solving the "Intermediary" through Proximity and Pedigree
By combining **Geographic Proximity** (Circles) with **Social Pedigree** (Invitation Lineage), KULA removes the need for a central "Security" or "Moderation" intermediary. The community self-moderates through the visibility of its own bonds. If you can see the path that connects you to a person, the friction of interaction vanishes.
